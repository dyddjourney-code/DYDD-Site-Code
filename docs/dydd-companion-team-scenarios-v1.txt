DYDD COMPANION TEAM SCENARIOS - FIRST TEACHING LIBRARY DRAFT

Purpose:
This document turns the eight DYDD companions into workshop-ready team scenarios. Each scenario is written as a teaching story that can be used in live training, blogs, website content, discussion guides, coaching sessions, or role-play exercises.

Source logic used:
- DesignID reflections: Architect = Creator, Artisan = Designer, Shepherd = Comforter, Steward = Sustainer.
- Integrative reflections: Visionary = Architect + Artisan; Interpreter = Artisan + Shepherd; Guardian = Shepherd + Steward; Overseer = Steward + Architect; Innovator = Architect + Shepherd; Craftsman = Artisan + Steward.
- Shadow reflection: what happens when a person's God-given design moves out of alignment with grace, humility, rest, or love.
- DesignPD tendencies: Plan = Dreamer/Doer, Decide = Feel It/Think It, Do = Solo/Together.
- Persona source: DYDD Companion Personas v1.

Character reference:
- Marcus: male Architect, secondary Steward, Overseer. Strong Doer, Think It, Solo. Shadow: control under pressure.
- Elena: female Architect, secondary Shepherd, Innovator. Dreamer, Feel It, Together. Shadow: pressure to rescue through vision.
- Leo: male Artisan, secondary Architect, Visionary. Dreamer, Think It, Solo. Shadow: restless perfectionism.
- Naomi: female Artisan, secondary Steward, Craftsman. Balanced Plan, Think It, Solo. Shadow: quiet overwork and hidden comparison.
- Daniel: male Shepherd, secondary Steward, Guardian. Doer, Feel It, Together. Shadow: rescuing and over-carrying.
- Mara: female Shepherd, secondary Artisan, Interpreter. Dreamer, Feel It, Together. Shadow: emotional absorption and over-interpretation.
- Miles: male Steward, secondary Architect, Overseer. Strong Doer, Think It, Balanced Do. Shadow: burdened responsibility.
- Clara: female Steward, secondary Shepherd, Guardian. Doer, Balanced Decide, Together. Shadow: approval-driven responsibility.

Facilitator note:
These scenarios are not meant to prove that one reflection is always right. They are designed to show how each reflection sees part of the truth, how shadows distort good instincts, and how teams become wiser when purpose, beauty, care, and stewardship are all invited into the room.


SCENARIO 1: THE WORKSHOP LAUNCH THAT ALMOST BECAME THREE DIFFERENT EVENTS

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for leadership teams, ministry launch planning, workshop design, or any group trying to turn vision into an actual event without losing people in the process.

Cast:
- Marcus: Architect/Steward, Overseer
- Leo: Artisan/Architect, Visionary
- Daniel: Shepherd/Steward, Guardian
- Clara: Steward/Shepherd, Guardian

Reflection mix:
This is a mostly balanced four-person team. Architect gives direction. Artisan gives message and experience. Shepherd protects people. Steward protects execution. It is a strong combination, but it can still fragment if each person starts building from their own instinct without naming the shared purpose.

Story:
A church asks the DYDD team to build a 90-minute introduction workshop in four weeks. The senior pastor wants it to be clear enough for new people, deep enough for mature believers, and simple enough that the church can run it again without outside help.

Marcus immediately starts sketching the structure. He sees four movements: identity, design, shadow, and next step. He wants outcomes, timing blocks, room setup, handouts, and a clear closing invitation.

Leo listens quietly, then says the workshop will fall flat if it feels like a class. He wants people to experience their design before they analyze it. He imagines reflection cards, visual language, a simple wall exercise, and a story arc that makes the four reflections memorable.

Daniel asks who will be in the room. He is thinking about the woman who already feels unseen, the retired man who thinks his purpose has passed him by, and the young adult who is afraid of being labeled. He wants space for prayer, gentle language, and a moment where people can share without being put on the spot.

Clara opens a spreadsheet. She asks who is registering people, who prints materials, how follow-up will happen, what the pastor receives after the event, and who owns next steps after people discover their reflection.

The meeting gets tense because everyone is right. Marcus thinks Leo is making the event too complex. Leo thinks Marcus is reducing transformation into a sequence. Daniel thinks both are moving too fast for the people in the room. Clara quietly sees that if they do not define ownership, the workshop may inspire people and then abandon them.

The integrated win:
They create a workshop that moves like a story but holds like a structure. Marcus keeps the teaching clear. Leo makes the reflection experience memorable. Daniel shapes the tone so people feel safe. Clara builds the follow-up path so the event becomes a beginning instead of a one-time moment.

The final workshop has:
- A simple opening story about identity before activity.
- Four reflection stations using color, symbols, and short phrases.
- A guided moment where people notice both gift and shadow without shame.
- A next-step card that connects each person to a follow-up conversation, small group, or ministry pathway.
- A reusable facilitator checklist so the church can run it again.

Problems approached:
- Vision without structure.
- Structure without emotional safety.
- Creativity without repeatability.
- Follow-up as an afterthought.
- The tension between a powerful one-time experience and a sustainable ministry pathway.

Wins:
- The workshop feels alive, not mechanical.
- People understand the four reflections quickly.
- The church receives a repeatable process.
- Follow-up is built before the event happens.
- Each reflection has a visible contribution.

Shadow friction:
- Marcus may tighten control when time is short.
- Leo may want one more creative layer even when the workshop is already strong.
- Daniel may overprotect participants from challenge.
- Clara may say yes to too many support tasks because she wants the event to succeed.

Teaching points:
- A balanced team still needs shared order. Balance does not remove the need for alignment.
- The Architect should not own all clarity. The Steward often sees operational clarity the Architect misses.
- The Artisan should not be treated as decorative. Beauty can be a form of wisdom when it helps people remember truth.
- The Shepherd should not be added only at the end for prayer. Relational care belongs in the design phase.

Facilitation questions:
- Which person would you naturally side with in the first meeting?
- Which voice would your team be most likely to rush past?
- Where does your own shadow appear when an event deadline gets close?
- What would have happened if Clara had not asked about follow-up?
- What would have happened if Daniel had not asked who was actually in the room?

Workshop exercise:
Give participants 12 minutes to redesign the workshop using only three roles. Then ask them what broke when one reflection was missing. Finally, let them add the fourth role back in and name what changed.

Transfer principle:
Healthy launch teams do not just ask, "What are we building?" They ask, "Will this be clear, meaningful, caring, and sustainable?"


SCENARIO 2: THE BURNED-OUT VOLUNTEER TEAM

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for churches, nonprofits, small businesses, and leadership teams that keep trying to recruit more people without asking why the people they already have are exhausted.

Cast:
- Mara: Shepherd/Artisan, Interpreter
- Daniel: Shepherd/Steward, Guardian
- Miles: Steward/Architect, Overseer
- Elena: Architect/Shepherd, Innovator
- Naomi: Artisan/Steward, Craftsman

Reflection mix:
This scenario intentionally carries two Shepherds. The doubled Shepherd presence gives the team a strong capacity for care, trust, and emotional interpretation. The risk is that the team can become so aware of pain that it hesitates to make hard structural changes.

Story:
A volunteer ministry is fraying. Three key leaders have stepped down, weekly attendance is inconsistent, and the remaining volunteers are smiling in public but quietly resentful in private. The pastor's first instinct is to host a recruitment Sunday.

Mara senses immediately that recruitment is not the real problem. She notices the tone in the room, the way people make jokes about being tired, and the quiet shame of leaders who feel like they are failing. She says, "People are not asking for more help because they do not believe help will change the pattern."

Daniel agrees. He has spoken with several volunteers who love the ministry but feel spiritually and emotionally drained. He wants listening meetings before strategy. He believes the team needs repentance, care, and honest language.

Miles studies the schedule and finds the hidden issue. The ministry has no rotation limits, no backup roles, no real onboarding, and no definition of what counts as a sustainable load. A few faithful people have been carrying invisible responsibility for years.

Elena wants to rebuild the entire volunteer pathway. She sees a new model: clear roles, shorter commitments, shadowing, monthly encouragement, and a pathway for people to serve from design rather than guilt.

Naomi takes the ideas and starts crafting usable tools. She writes role cards, a one-page volunteer covenant, a simple training outline, and a leader check-in rhythm that sounds human instead of corporate.

The integrated win:
The team stops treating burnout as a recruiting problem and starts treating it as a discipleship, stewardship, and design problem. They build a volunteer model that protects people while still inviting sacrifice.

The final solution has:
- A listening week before any recruitment push.
- A role audit that names hidden labor.
- Service lanes connected to DesignID reflections.
- Rotation limits and sabbath rhythms.
- A training guide that explains expectations and spiritual purpose.
- A pastoral care plan for tired volunteers.

Problems approached:
- Burnout masked as low volunteer commitment.
- Guilt-based service.
- Lack of role clarity.
- Over-functioning Shepherds and Stewards.
- Leaders confusing faithfulness with constant availability.

Wins:
- Volunteers feel seen before they are asked for more.
- Leaders discover the real system problem.
- New volunteers enter clearer roles.
- Existing volunteers are honored, not simply replaced.
- The ministry becomes more sustainable and more relational.

Shadow friction:
- Mara may absorb everyone else's pain and feel responsible for healing it.
- Daniel may over-carry conversations that should become shared leadership responsibility.
- Miles may sound too blunt when naming limits.
- Elena may want to rebuild everything immediately.
- Naomi may quietly overwork to make the new tools excellent.

Teaching points:
- Compassion without stewardship repeats burnout.
- Stewardship without compassion sounds cold.
- Innovation should not outrun trust.
- Good volunteer systems protect love from becoming exhaustion.
- Sometimes the most spiritual thing a team can do is define a limit.

Facilitation questions:
- Where have you seen recruitment used to avoid repair?
- Which role would be hardest for your team to receive: Mara's empathy, Miles's limits, Elena's redesign, or Naomi's tools?
- What invisible labor exists in your current team or ministry?
- What would change if volunteers served from design instead of guilt?

Workshop exercise:
Ask participants to list every invisible task inside one real ministry or team. Then have them mark each task as relational care, creative communication, strategic direction, or operational stewardship. Discuss which category is overloaded.

Transfer principle:
Burnout is often not a people shortage first. It is a design, expectation, and care problem.


SCENARIO 3: THE WEBSITE REDESIGN THAT BECAME A THEOLOGY FIGHT

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for communications teams, church staff, founders, ministry brands, and anyone who has seen a design conversation become a mission conversation.

Cast:
- Leo: Artisan/Architect, Visionary
- Naomi: Artisan/Steward, Craftsman
- Marcus: Architect/Steward, Overseer
- Elena: Architect/Shepherd, Innovator
- Miles: Steward/Architect, Overseer

Reflection mix:
This team has two Artisans and two Architects, with a strong Steward presence. It is full of clarity, strategy, design skill, and responsibility. It does not include a primary Shepherd, which becomes the lesson.

Story:
The team is asked to redesign the DYDD homepage. The current page explains the program, but people are not staying long enough to understand it. Everyone agrees the page needs to change. They do not agree on what the change means.

Leo wants the page to feel like an awakening. He wants fewer words, stronger visuals, more emotional sequencing, and a first impression that makes people think, "This is about me."

Naomi likes Leo's direction but worries that beauty without practice will not help the visitor. She wants cleaner section language, fewer abstractions, and small examples that show what a person actually does next.

Marcus wants the page to move visitors through a clear decision pathway. He sketches the hierarchy: problem, promise, pathway, proof, invitation. He wants the site to serve a measurable purpose.

Elena pushes for a bolder headline. She believes the page should not just explain DYDD; it should call people into purpose. She wants the page to feel courageous and spiritually alive.

Miles asks what claims can be supported. He wants clear boundaries around transformation language, pricing, program expectations, and what happens after someone submits an assessment.

The conversation gets sharp. Leo thinks Miles is draining the page of wonder. Miles thinks Leo and Elena are drifting toward overpromise. Marcus thinks the team needs to decide the funnel before debating tone. Naomi sees that everyone is using different definitions of "clear."

The integrated win:
They create a homepage that is beautiful, truthful, structured, and responsible. It does not merely market DYDD. It helps a real visitor recognize need, understand the pathway, and take a next faithful step.

The final page direction includes:
- A first section centered on identity and divine design.
- A short story-based problem statement.
- Four reflection signals using the DYDD colors.
- A simple "how the journey works" section.
- Honest language about assessment, reflection, coaching, and growth.
- A call to action that invites curiosity instead of pressure.

Problems approached:
- Beauty versus accuracy.
- Vision versus proof.
- Conversion versus care.
- Different meanings of clarity.
- The absence of a primary Shepherd voice.

Wins:
- The team avoids both hype and dryness.
- The design serves the theology instead of distracting from it.
- The pathway becomes clear enough for strangers.
- The copy honors both promise and limits.
- The team discovers why a Shepherd review is needed before launch.

Shadow friction:
- Leo may chase the perfect emotional moment.
- Naomi may keep refining details because she sees every weak phrase.
- Marcus may reduce the page to a funnel.
- Elena may overreach in the name of calling people higher.
- Miles may protect accuracy so strongly that invitation loses warmth.

Teaching points:
- A page can be strategically correct and still feel unsafe.
- A beautiful message can still overpromise.
- The Shepherd question is essential: "How will this land on someone who is afraid, weary, skeptical, or ashamed?"
- Strong creative teams need truth checks; strong strategic teams need humanity checks.

Facilitation questions:
- Who do you become in a disagreement about message: Leo, Naomi, Marcus, Elena, or Miles?
- What does your organization overvalue: beauty, structure, boldness, accuracy, or simplicity?
- Where might a missing Shepherd voice change the final product?
- What promise are you tempted to make because you believe in the work?

Workshop exercise:
Give each table a fictional homepage headline. Ask them to review it from each reflection: Architect, Artisan, Shepherd, Steward. Then have them rewrite it so it is compelling, clear, caring, and honest.

Transfer principle:
The best message is not the loudest promise. It is the clearest invitation that love and truth can carry together.


SCENARIO 4: THE NEW MEMBER PATHWAY

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for churches, onboarding teams, membership systems, discipleship groups, and organizational culture work.

Cast:
- Clara: Steward/Shepherd, Guardian
- Mara: Shepherd/Artisan, Interpreter
- Leo: Artisan/Architect, Visionary
- Marcus: Architect/Steward, Overseer

Reflection mix:
This is a clean four-reflection team. Each person brings a needed lens: process, care, communication, and direction. The tension is not whether the team has the right ingredients, but whether they will honor the order in which people experience belonging.

Story:
A growing church wants a new member pathway. The old process is one class, one form, and one follow-up email. People join, but many still feel unknown six months later.

Marcus sees the pathway in phases: welcome, orientation, discovery, connection, serving, and belonging. He wants milestones and measurable handoffs.

Clara sees the operational gaps. Nobody owns the first contact after the class. The database tags are inconsistent. Ministry leaders do not receive enough information to follow up well. She knows that if the system stays vague, people will slip through.

Mara listens to stories from recent members and hears something deeper. People do not just need steps. They need to know when they become more than visitors. They need language for the emotional move from attending to belonging.

Leo designs the pathway as a visible journey. He creates simple symbols and names for each step, writes warmer copy, and turns the process into something people can understand without feeling processed.

The integrated win:
The church creates a new member pathway that is structured enough to be dependable and relational enough to feel pastoral. It becomes a journey of belonging, not a conveyor belt.

The final pathway includes:
- A welcome conversation before the class.
- A membership session with story, doctrine, design, and next step.
- A post-class connection guide.
- Ministry placement based partly on DesignID reflection.
- Follow-up reminders that prompt real conversations, not just automated emails.
- A 60-day check-in that asks, "Do you feel known, connected, and clear about your next step?"

Problems approached:
- Assimilation without belonging.
- Systems that track people but do not know them.
- Care that depends on memory instead of process.
- Creative language that could become too clever if not anchored.
- Strategic pathways that can feel like machinery.

Wins:
- New members understand the path.
- Staff and volunteers know who owns each handoff.
- People are connected by design, not only availability.
- Follow-up becomes both reliable and personal.
- The church gains a repeatable belonging rhythm.

Shadow friction:
- Clara may feel responsible for every missed detail.
- Mara may resist any structure that feels impersonal.
- Leo may over-brand the path.
- Marcus may move too quickly into milestones before sitting with the emotional experience of newcomers.

Teaching points:
- A pathway is not automatically impersonal. A well-designed pathway can protect belonging.
- Shepherds often identify the emotional truth of a process.
- Stewards often identify the trust gaps in a process.
- Architects help the team see the whole journey.
- Artisans help people remember and enter the journey.

Facilitation questions:
- Where does your current onboarding process create clarity?
- Where does it create confusion or distance?
- Who owns the moment when a person moves from attendee to known participant?
- Which reflection would improve your current pathway first?

Workshop exercise:
Have each table map a real onboarding journey from first contact to meaningful belonging. Use four colored marks: direction, meaning, care, and follow-through. Discuss where the map is strong and where it is thin.

Transfer principle:
Belonging needs both a human touch and a dependable path.


SCENARIO 5: THE YOUTH OUTREACH BUDGET CUT

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for budget conversations, ministry strategy, nonprofit leadership, board training, and faith-versus-wisdom discussions.

Cast:
- Miles: Steward/Architect, Overseer
- Elena: Architect/Shepherd, Innovator
- Daniel: Shepherd/Steward, Guardian
- Naomi: Artisan/Steward, Craftsman

Reflection mix:
This team has one strong Steward, one strong Architect, one strong Shepherd, and one strong Artisan. The Steward voice is especially central because the scenario deals with limited resources.

Story:
A youth outreach initiative loses 35 percent of its budget halfway through the planning season. The team had planned a larger event series with food, transportation, guest speakers, worship, and follow-up groups. Now the numbers do not work.

Daniel reacts first from the people side. He knows the students by name. He knows which families are fragile, which kids need transportation, and which volunteers have already promised to show up. He does not want the budget cut to become an excuse to pull back from vulnerable students.

Miles asks for the actual numbers. He is not cold, but he is clear. The team cannot spend money it does not have. If they overextend now, they may damage trust later.

Elena sees a different model. Instead of one large event series, she proposes a smaller neighborhood-based approach: fewer big nights, more host homes, trained mentors, and a simple discipleship path.

Naomi turns the new model into a beautiful low-cost kit. It includes conversation cards, a host guide, a student reflection sheet, simple worship playlists, and a testimony framework that volunteers can use without needing to be professional teachers.

The integrated win:
The budget cut becomes a design constraint rather than a mission killer. The team moves from expensive event dependency to a more relational, reproducible outreach model.

The final approach includes:
- One anchor gathering instead of four large events.
- Neighborhood groups hosted by trusted adults.
- A transportation priority list for students most in need.
- A mentor guide and student reflection kit.
- Clear budget guardrails.
- A testimony night where students share what God is doing.

Problems approached:
- Scarcity thinking.
- Emotional attachment to the original plan.
- Stewardship misunderstood as lack of faith.
- Innovation that could move too fast for volunteers.
- Quality concerns with low-cost resources.

Wins:
- The outreach remains alive.
- The most vulnerable students stay prioritized.
- The budget becomes honest and sustainable.
- Volunteers gain ownership.
- The ministry model becomes easier to repeat.

Shadow friction:
- Miles may sound like he is shutting down faith when he is trying to protect trust.
- Daniel may equate cutting scope with abandoning people.
- Elena may underestimate how much support the new model requires.
- Naomi may over-polish the kit to compensate for lower production value.

Teaching points:
- Faith does not require pretending constraints are not real.
- Stewardship can protect mission from collapse.
- Shepherd care helps teams remember who is affected by numbers.
- Architect innovation can turn limitation into a new path.
- Artisan craft can make a smaller model feel intentional rather than cheap.

Facilitation questions:
- What do you do first when a plan loses resources?
- Which voice in this scenario feels most spiritual to you? Which feels least spiritual?
- Where have you mistaken scale for fruitfulness?
- What would be worth preserving even if the model changed?

Workshop exercise:
Give each group a ministry budget cut and ask them to redesign the plan while preserving the mission. Require each group to name one thing they will protect, one thing they will release, one thing they will simplify, and one thing they will measure.

Transfer principle:
Stewardship is not the enemy of faith. It is one way faithfulness learns to endure.


SCENARIO 6: THE BEAUTIFUL RESOURCE NOBODY USES

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for product development, curriculum design, discipleship tools, internal training, and teams that confuse quality with adoption.

Cast:
- Leo: Artisan/Architect, Visionary
- Naomi: Artisan/Steward, Craftsman
- Clara: Steward/Shepherd, Guardian
- Daniel: Shepherd/Steward, Guardian

Reflection mix:
This scenario intentionally has two Artisans and two Guardians. It has strong craft, care, and follow-through, but no primary Architect. The missing Architect becomes visible when the team struggles to connect the resource to a compelling larger purpose.

Story:
The team creates a discipleship resource for small groups. It is visually excellent, doctrinally thoughtful, and well organized. The first few groups receive it warmly. Three months later, almost nobody is using it.

Leo is frustrated. He believes the resource is strong and suspects leaders did not understand how to use it. He wants to improve the presentation and create a better launch video.

Naomi reads the material again and sees possible refinements. Some lessons ask too much in one sitting. A few prompts are beautiful but not practical. She wants to improve the tool before judging the users.

Clara looks at the implementation trail. There was no owner after launch, no reminder rhythm, no leader check-in, and no feedback loop. The resource was handed out, not shepherded.

Daniel talks with group leaders. They are not rejecting the tool. They are intimidated by it. Some feel unqualified to lead deeper conversations. Others do not know what to do when someone gives a vulnerable answer.

The integrated win:
The team stops treating adoption as a design-only problem. They rebuild the resource as a guided practice with a relational on-ramp, a simpler first use, and a follow-through rhythm.

The revised approach includes:
- A four-week starter version.
- A leader confidence guide.
- A one-page "what to do when the conversation gets real" sheet.
- A monthly reminder and testimony prompt.
- A simple feedback form.
- A short orientation call for group leaders.

Problems approached:
- Assuming quality creates use.
- Over-designing before testing.
- Launch without ownership.
- Users feeling intimidated by spiritual depth.
- Lack of a clear larger why.

Wins:
- Leaders begin using the resource.
- The tool becomes simpler without becoming shallow.
- Feedback shapes the next version.
- Group leaders feel supported, not evaluated.
- The team learns that implementation is part of design.

Shadow friction:
- Leo may defend the original creative vision.
- Naomi may keep improving instead of releasing.
- Clara may carry the entire follow-up system herself.
- Daniel may soften accountability because leaders feel insecure.

Teaching points:
- A resource is not complete until someone can actually use it.
- Artisans often see quality gaps; Stewards see ownership gaps; Shepherds see confidence gaps.
- Missing Architect energy can leave the team without a compelling rally point.
- Adoption requires design, trust, repetition, and purpose.

Facilitation questions:
- Where have you created something strong that people did not use?
- Was the issue quality, clarity, confidence, ownership, or purpose?
- Which team member names the problem you usually miss?
- What is the smallest first-use version of your current resource?

Workshop exercise:
Ask participants to choose one unused tool, document, or process in their organization. Have them diagnose it through four lenses: purpose, usability, emotional safety, and follow-through. Then write one adoption experiment that can be tested in two weeks.

Transfer principle:
Usable beats impressive when transformation is the goal.


SCENARIO 7: THE STRATEGIC RETREAT WITH TOO MUCH WEIGHT IN THE ROOM

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for senior teams, boards, pastors, founders, and groups leading after a hard season.

Cast:
- Marcus: Architect/Steward, Overseer
- Elena: Architect/Shepherd, Innovator
- Miles: Steward/Architect, Overseer
- Daniel: Shepherd/Steward, Guardian
- Mara: Shepherd/Artisan, Interpreter

Reflection mix:
This scenario carries two Architects, two Shepherds, and a Steward. It is strong in future, responsibility, and care. It does not include a primary Artisan, so the team struggles to translate heavy truth into a memorable shared story.

Story:
A leadership team gathers for a two-day retreat after a difficult year. They have lost staff, disappointed some members, and delayed several initiatives. The goal is to discern the next year of ministry.

Marcus arrives with a planning grid. He believes the team needs to leave with three priorities, owners, dates, and decision rules. He wants hope, but he also wants traction.

Elena brings future energy. She believes the pain of the last year could become the ground for a more courageous season. She wants the team to dream again and stop managing decline.

Miles brings a sober dashboard. Giving is down, trust is uneven, and staff capacity is thin. He believes the next plan must be smaller, clearer, and more honest.

Daniel names the people who are tired. He worries that another strategic push could feel like leaders asking wounded people to run harder.

Mara names the grief beneath the room. She notices that the team keeps jumping to next steps whenever loss is mentioned. She says, "We cannot build a faithful future by stepping over what people are still carrying."

The first day feels heavy. Marcus worries they are not making progress. Elena worries the room is losing courage. Miles worries no one wants to face reality. Daniel and Mara worry the team is about to spiritualize exhaustion.

The integrated win:
The team creates a retreat rhythm that holds lament and leadership together. They spend the first half-day telling the truth about the year, praying, and naming what must be released. Only then do they move into future planning.

The final retreat outcome includes:
- A shared lament statement.
- A list of what the team will stop carrying.
- Three strategic priorities instead of seven.
- A capacity filter for every initiative.
- A communication plan for the church.
- A pastoral follow-up rhythm for staff and key volunteers.

Problems approached:
- Strategy used to avoid grief.
- Grief used to avoid decisions.
- Financial pressure.
- Vision fatigue.
- The missing Artisan voice that could help the team name the season with language people remember.

Wins:
- The team tells the truth without losing hope.
- The next-year plan is smaller and stronger.
- Staff capacity is protected.
- People feel spiritually cared for, not managed.
- The team leaves with both clarity and tenderness.

Shadow friction:
- Marcus may push for action before the room is ready.
- Elena may use vision to escape pain.
- Miles may over-focus on risk.
- Daniel may protect people from necessary challenge.
- Mara may feel the grief so deeply that decision-making feels harsh.

Teaching points:
- After a hard season, clarity without lament can feel violent.
- Lament without leadership can become stuck.
- Stewards help teams tell the truth about capacity.
- Architects help teams recover direction.
- Shepherds help teams honor the human cost of the journey.
- A missing Artisan voice often shows up as a lack of shared language, symbol, or story.

Facilitation questions:
- Does your team tend to rush grief or sit in it too long?
- Which person in the scenario would frustrate you most?
- What does faithful leadership require after a loss?
- What phrase, image, or story would help people carry the new direction?

Workshop exercise:
Ask participants to write two lists for a real team: "What we must honor" and "What we must now build." Then have them create a one-sentence retreat theme that can carry both.

Transfer principle:
Leadership after loss must make room for both tears and traction.


SCENARIO 8: THE QUIET FAILURE OF FOLLOW-THROUGH

Teaching use:
Use this scenario for operations, ministry execution, leadership development, ADHD-friendly planning, and teams that launch well but drift later.

Cast:
- Clara: Steward/Shepherd, Guardian
- Miles: Steward/Architect, Overseer
- Mara: Shepherd/Artisan, Interpreter
- Leo: Artisan/Architect, Visionary

Reflection mix:
This scenario carries two Stewards, one Shepherd, and one Artisan. It is strong in responsibility, care, and meaning. It lacks a primary Architect, which creates a subtle problem: the team can improve follow-through without renewing the future that makes follow-through worth it.

Story:
A church launched a new mentoring initiative six months ago. The launch Sunday was strong. The testimonies were moving. Sign-ups were high. Now only a handful of mentor pairs are still meeting consistently.

Clara sees the missed handoffs first. No one was assigned to check in with mentors after week four. The matching spreadsheet is outdated. Some participants never received next-step emails. She feels the weight of every detail that fell through.

Miles sees the ownership issue. The team created enthusiasm but not a durable operating model. There are no review dates, no leader dashboard, no escalation pathway, and no clear definition of success.

Mara listens to mentors and hears embarrassment. People are not only busy; they feel like they failed. Some pairs missed meetings and did not know how to restart. Others hit awkward conversations and quietly backed away.

Leo sees that the message has gone stale. The launch story was compelling, but nobody kept reminding the church why mentoring mattered. He believes the initiative needs fresh testimony, better language, and a visible rhythm.

The integrated win:
The team rebuilds follow-through as a combination of responsibility, encouragement, and renewed purpose. They do not shame people back into consistency. They give them a path to restart.

The repair plan includes:
- A mentor restart email with grace-filled language.
- A simple monthly check-in form.
- A dashboard for active, paused, and needs-support pairs.
- A testimony rhythm every six weeks.
- A mentor support night focused on real obstacles.
- A clear owner for follow-up.

Problems approached:
- Strong launch, weak sustainment.
- Shame after inconsistency.
- Lack of visible ownership.
- Follow-through reduced to task reminders.
- Missing future language.

Wins:
- Mentor pairs restart without shame.
- Leaders can see the true status.
- The ministry gains a sustainable review rhythm.
- Testimonies renew purpose.
- Clara and Miles stop carrying invisible anxiety because ownership becomes clear.

Shadow friction:
- Clara may feel personally responsible for the drift.
- Miles may over-correct with too much reporting.
- Mara may hesitate to name accountability because people already feel ashamed.
- Leo may want to refresh the brand before fixing the operating rhythm.

Teaching points:
- Follow-through is not just discipline. It is sustained meaning plus visible responsibility.
- Stewards are often the first to feel drift because they sense what has been entrusted.
- Shepherds can help accountability sound like restoration instead of accusation.
- Artisans can refresh the story so people remember why the work matters.
- Missing Architect energy may leave the repair plan practical but not future-charged.

Facilitation questions:
- Where has your team launched with energy but failed to sustain?
- What was missing: ownership, rhythm, care, story, or direction?
- How can accountability become an invitation rather than a shame trigger?
- What is one visible signal that would tell you the initiative is healthy again?

Workshop exercise:
Ask participants to choose one initiative that has drifted. Have them design a 30-day restart plan with four parts: renewed purpose, people care, ownership rhythm, and evidence of progress.

Transfer principle:
Teams do not drift because they hate the mission. They drift when purpose, ownership, care, and rhythm stop reinforcing each other.


LIBRARY-WIDE TEACHING FRAMEWORK

Use this framework after any scenario:

1. Name the first instinct.
Ask each participant which character they understood first. This reveals their natural reflection bias.

2. Name the missing truth.
Ask what the team would lose if one reflection were absent. This trains people to value difference before conflict begins.

3. Name the shadow.
Ask where a good gift became distorted under pressure. This keeps the conversation from becoming personality flattery.

4. Name the integration.
Ask what became possible only when the team honored multiple reflections together.

5. Name the next faithful action.
Ask what one decision, rhythm, phrase, handoff, or boundary would help the team move from insight to obedience.
